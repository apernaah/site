FYI: This is going to be a long one.

I initially didn't have any plans to go home for my birthday, but then my friend Riya mentioned she was spending her 25th birthday here in the UK. So, I booked an Emirates flight from Stansted to Kochi to visit my family for three weeks instead. I arrived Kochi on the morning of January 29th and immediately demolished a breakfast of porotta, beef, and chicken (because I can), all washed down with coffee.

On a quick side note: 

Issue 1: The traffic police at the Edappally and Maradu junctions are entirely botching the traffic flow. They turned off the automatic signals to manage things manually, and honestly, it was a disaster. Words can't do it justice; you just have to see the chaos for yourself. It would make so much more sense to let the automatic signals do their job and have the same police focus on catching traffic violators.

Issue 2: I noticed a bunch of crosswalks (see puthiya road junction after Decathlon in Kalamassery for example), but absolutely no way to actually cross the median. It was either blocked by a post or simply never paved into a pedestrian path. But whyy

Anyway, I went home to crash. The next day, I woke up and attended the Jain Future Summit for a bit, then went for a drive with my brother and sister-in-law. For the next few days, I fell into a lazy routine: sleeping in late and heading out with friends in the evenings.

Because my family is Hindu, they have a strict belief against cutting hair during my birth month. I knew that meant no trips to the salon until at least February 12th. Then I had to defend my hair for 5 days because my flight back home is on 17th night. I fully expected my mom to bring up my transition but she had only chances when dad's not near, and sure enough she interrogated me about it every single night. I brushed it off. My main goal is to be myself without hurting them, which seems to be exactly what my brother is worried about too (not to make my mom and dad worry). It’s a whole chain reaction of anxiety: my sister-in-law worries about my brother, my brother worries about mom, and mom worries about dad. Dad is still completely in the dark, and frankly, I have zero plans to tell him anytime soon.

As the days dragged on, my mom continuously badgered me. "Had I changed my mind yet? Was I going to burn all my dresses and feminine clothes?" She insisted I needed to start "thinking like a man." At one point, she flat-out told me she doesn't care if being myself makes me happy; apparently, my only source of happiness should come from pleasing the family. She capped it off with, "We raised you as a male, so you should live as a male."
She even claimed I was possessed by a spirit named Aparna and threatened to take me to an exorcist. When I asked if she actually believed in that nonsense, she admitted she didn't, but wanted to "experiment." I wasn't worried, though. She had already complained that an exorcism would cost 50k INR minimum, money she noted could be invested elsewhere. Classic.

On February 10th, I visited my younger cousins (I only have three younger than me, everyone else is older: two on my dad's side, one on my mom's). I’ve been incredibly close to the eldest of the two since we were kids. She, along with many of my mentees, has always looked up to me, so I was really hesitant about coming out to her. Still, the urge was overwhelming. I asked her to see if she would be free to chat online later that evening, planning to drop the news.

Later that night, I took the plunge and sent her one of my girlmod selfies (you know the ones, with the phone covering half my face). She was stunned: "Is that... are you sure?" I poured my heart out. At first, she asked if it was some sort of illness, which confused me a bit, but she quickly followed up with, "If not, it's totally fine." Once I explained my gender dysphoria, it clicked for her. "So you're not just doing this for fun... you are Aparna. Wow."
I sent her a bunch of my photos, and she gushed over how pretty I looked. I walked her through my whole situation and shared my blogs. When she asked about my nicknames, I rattled off Apps, Appsy (from Prachi), Appy Fizz (from Ananya and Sayantika), and Happsy. But after seeing my Hello Kitty pajamas, she immediately dubbed me Kitty Kat. Kitty, Kat, and Katty. We spent hours gossiping about sarees, dresses, and bodyshape. She switched to exclusively feminine pronouns, instantly changing how she addressed me from mon (masc) to mol (fem). We were texting exactly like two girls. Eventually, she had to crash because, unlike me, she actually had to be at work in eight hours. Before logging off, she hyped me up, demanding more pictures and telling me to go out in London dressed however I wanted, without caring what anyone else thought.

The next morning, out of nowhere, my mom woke me up and dragged me to a popular sexologist in Edappally. I honestly knew this day was coming; she’d been plotting it ever since she first got suspicious. She used to grill me about my attractions. I'd told her I wasn't sexually attracted to anyone (though internally, I am to men) and romantically attracted to both (though I'm solely romantically attracted to women). It’s an exhausting concept to explain to someone, especially since I would only ever take on a feminine role in intimacy. I just couldn't get her to grasp that sexuality has nothing to do with my gender identity.

We arrived at 8:00 AM. The clinic didn't even open until 8:30, making us their very first patients. While waiting, I scoped out the place and noticed they had a staff psychologist who happened to be on leave, while the clinic's owner and head doctor was also a psychologist. At 9:00 AM, a junior doctor called me in to take my history. Right on cue, my mom started bawling in front of them, begging them to "make me a man." The doctor needed me to open up about my physical and mental state, so my mom had to leave the room before I could spill the tea. I explained my reality to the junior doctor, who kindly admitted it was out of their hands but promised to help however they could. By 9:30, we were told to hang tight until the senior doctor arrived at 10:00.
When the senior doctor finally showed up, he called me into the consulting room alone. Surprisingly, he was a pretty chill guy. He simply asked me who I was and what I wanted. I looked at him and said I am Aparna. I explained my body dysphoria, my love for feminine clothes, and the sheer euphoria I feel when younger people call me Chechi (sister). I laid it all out: I want to live my life as a woman, but I desperately want to avoid breaking my family's hearts. He asked if I planned on getting a vaginoplasty. I told him no partly because I don't want the surgical risks, and partly because, let's be real, it's incredibly expensive.

Eventually, they brought my mom in. Cue the waterworks again. She launched into a monologue about how they raised me as a boy, begging the doctor to pump me full of testosterone hormones to "make me a man" and desperately asking if conversion therapy would work. To my absolute shock, the doctor shut it down. He told my mom point-blank that she needed to accept that transgender people walk among us, and that I am a transfem. He mentioned their in-house psychologist, and when I pointed out I’d noticed she was off duty that day, he seemed genuinely impressed by my observation skills and my nerdy ability to rattle off my ID card numbers from memory without having the physical card on me.

I'm not entirely sure if he was just honoring my request to soften the blow for my family, but he reassured my mom by telling her she didn't need to panic since I "could change back anytime," and reiterated my promise about skipping the surgery. He was brutally honest with her, explaining that injecting me with unnecessary testosterone was dangerous, and definitively stated that conversion therapy is a scam. He warned her that charlatans would happily take her money just to give her false hope of "making me a man." The disappointment on her face was heavy. Honestly, it wasn't just her disappointment I was seeing; she was mirroring the collective disappointment of my dad, our extended family, and society as a whole.

Leading up to this, she always claimed she'd make peace with it if a medical professional explicitly told her I couldn't be "changed into a man." Yet, the aftermath of the consultation proved otherwise. Instead of acceptance, she was just angrier and sadder, snapping that the doctor didn't know anything and that she refused to believe him. She even demanded I start chanting I am a man on repeat. We were walking to the metro station (her heading to work and me heading home in opposite directions). I was so incredibly fed up that right before we parted ways, I asked:

"Mom, can I ask you something if you don’t mind?"

She: "What is it?" she sighed.

Me: "Where can I buy a really nice saree?"

She: "Oh my god, just kill me already."

(Long story short about the saree, I bought two sarees from Kalyan Silks and two blouses from amazon)

This post got way too long, so I think it's time to wrap up this episode here.
